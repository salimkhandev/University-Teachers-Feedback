                              ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The University Teachers Feedback System is a comprehensive, AI-powered web 
application designed to streamline the collection, analysis, and visualization of 
student feedback regarding university faculty members. Traditional feedback 
mechanisms in educational institutions often suffer from inefficiencies such as 
paper-based processes, lack of anonymity, delayed results, absence of actionable 
insights, and minimal engagement between teachers and the feedback they receive. 
This system addresses these challenges by providing a digital platform where 
students can submit feedback on their teachers through an intuitive user 
interface, administrators can monitor the entire ecosystem, and teachers can 
receive AI-generated summaries, interactive rating visualizations, and chatbot 
coaching based on student comments.

The system employs a modern technology stack comprising React with Vite for the 
frontend, Node.js with Express for the backend, MongoDB with Mongoose for data 
persistence, and Google Gemini artificial intelligence for natural language 
processing tasks. Role-based access control ensures that students, teachers, and 
administrators each have appropriate views and permissions. The platform 
incorporates intelligent caching strategies to optimize AI API usage, CSV bulk 
import capabilities for rapid data population, and comprehensive analytics 
dashboard with statistical charts. This thesis presents the complete system 
architecture, database schema design, implementation decisions, feature 
specifications, and deployment considerations of this production-ready 
educational technology solution.

1.1 Background and Motivation

In the contemporary landscape of higher education, the quality of teaching 
directly influences student learning outcomes, institutional reputation, and 
overall academic excellence. Universities worldwide have recognized the critical 
importance of collecting and analyzing student feedback as a mechanism for 
continuous improvement in teaching quality. However, the process of gathering, 
processing, and deriving actionable insights from student feedback has 
traditionally been fraught with challenges that span administrative, 
pedagogical, and technological domains.

Historically, many universities have relied on paper-based feedback forms 
distributed at the end of each academic term. These paper-based systems present 
numerous inefficiencies: they are resource-intensive to print and distribute, 
require manual data entry for analysis, are susceptible to data loss and 
physical damage, and provide limited anonymity which may discourage honest 
student responses. The logistical burden of managing thousands of paper forms 
across multiple departments and semesters is substantial, requiring significant 
staff time for distribution, collection, collation, and analysis. Furthermore, 
the time lag between feedback collection and result dissemination often renders 
the insights less actionable, as the academic term may have already concluded 
by the time teachers receive their evaluations.

Even when institutions have transitioned to digital solutions, many existing 
systems remain basic in their functionality, offering little more than simple 
form submission and basic statistical aggregation. These systems typically 
provide mean scores and frequency distributions but fail to extract meaningful 
insights from the qualitative comments that constitute the richest source of 
feedback data. The inability to automatically analyze text-based feedback 
represents a significant missed opportunity, as student comments often contain 
nuanced observations about teaching methodology, classroom environment, 
communication effectiveness, and subject matter expertise that cannot be 
captured through numerical ratings alone.

The motivation behind this project stems from the recognition that modern 
educational institutions require sophisticated feedback management systems that 
leverage contemporary technologies to provide meaningful, actionable insights. 
Artificial intelligence, in particular, has matured to a point where it can 
significantly enhance the analysis of qualitative feedback data. Natural 
language processing models can now summarize large volumes of student comments, 
identify common themes, detect sentiment patterns, extract key strengths and 
weaknesses, and generate coherent, professional summaries that would otherwise 
require hours of manual effort by administrators. This capability transforms 
what was previously a labor-intensive manual process into an automated, 
instantaneous, and consistent analysis pipeline.

Furthermore, the shift towards digital-native learning environments, accelerated 
by global trends in remote and hybrid education, has created an expectation 
among students that institutional processes should be as seamless and 
intelligent as the consumer applications they use daily. Students who have grown 
up with social media, e-commerce platforms, and on-demand services expect 
intuitive interfaces, real-time feedback, and personalized experiences from 
every digital interaction. Similarly, faculty members expect tools that provide 
them with genuine, constructive insights rather than raw data dumps that require 
further interpretation. Administrators need comprehensive dashboards that enable 
data-driven decision-making at departmental and institutional levels, allowing 
them to identify trends, address concerns proactively, and allocate resources 
effectively.

The specific challenges that motivated this project include the fragmentation of 
feedback tools across different institutional stakeholders, the absence of 
intelligent analysis for qualitative feedback data, the lack of integrated 
progress tracking for feedback completion, the complexity of managing 
institutional hierarchies spanning departments, semesters, sections, and 
subjects, and the need for bulk data import capabilities to handle the scale of 
university populations. Each of these challenges was carefully analyzed during 
the requirements gathering phase to ensure that the resulting system would 
provide a comprehensive solution rather than addressing only a subset of the 
problem space.

The University Teachers Feedback System was conceived to address these 
multifaceted requirements through a unified platform that serves all three 
stakeholder groups within a single, integrated application. By combining a 
modern web application architecture with Google's Gemini artificial 
intelligence, the system aims to transform the student feedback process from a 
bureaucratic requirement into a valuable component of the educational quality 
assurance ecosystem. The system aspires to close the feedback loop, ensuring 
that student voices are heard, teachers receive actionable insights, and 
administrators have the data they need to make informed decisions about faculty 
development and institutional improvement.

1.2 Problem Statement

Educational institutions face several critical challenges in implementing 
effective teacher feedback systems:

First, the lack of integrated platforms forces institutions to use disjointed 
tools for different aspects of the feedback process. A student may submit 
feedback through one system, while teachers view results through another, and 
administrators compile reports using yet another tool. This fragmentation leads 
to data inconsistencies, duplication of effort, and delayed insights.

Second, feedback analysis is overwhelmingly manual. When hundreds or thousands 
of student comments are collected, extracting meaningful patterns, identifying 
areas for improvement, and generating coherent summaries requires significant 
human effort. This often results in feedback data being underutilized or 
completely ignored due to resource constraints.

Third, the feedback loop between students, teachers, and administrators is often 
broken. Students may not see the impact of their feedback, teachers may not 
receive timely or actionable information, and administrators lack the tools to 
effectively monitor and improve teaching quality across departments.

Fourth, data management and security concerns arise when educational data is 
handled through disparate systems. Student information, teacher evaluations, and 
institutional data require proper access controls, secure storage, and 
compliance with data protection regulations.

Fifth, system setup and population present significant barriers to adoption. 
Universities have complex organizational structures with multiple departments, 
semesters, sections, subjects, and hundreds or thousands of students and 
teachers. Manually entering this data is prohibitive, yet many existing systems 
lack effective bulk import capabilities.

The University Teachers Feedback System addresses all these challenges through 
a unified, AI-enhanced platform that streamlines the entire feedback lifecycle 
from data collection to intelligent analysis and visualization.

1. To design and implement a comprehensive digital platform for collecting,

2. To integrate Google Gemini artificial intelligence for automatic generation

3. To implement role-based access control with distinct interfaces and 
   capabilities for students, teachers, and administrators.

4. To provide intuitive data visualization through charts, graphs, and analytics

5. To develop a bulk data import system that allows rapid population of the

6. To create an intelligent chatbot interface that allows teachers to interact 
   conversationally with their feedback data through AI-powered coaching.

7. To implement caching strategies that optimize API usage and reduce latency 
   for frequently accessed AI-generated content.

8. To provide comprehensive student tracking capabilities that allow 
   administrators to monitor feedback submission progress across departments, 
   semesters, and sections.

9. To design a modular, maintainable architecture that facilitates future 
   enhancements and scalability.

10. To deploy the system in a production environment using modern cloud 
    infrastructure.

1.4 Scope and Limitations

The scope of this project encompasses the complete development of a web-based 
University Teachers Feedback System with the following coverage:

The system supports three distinct user roles: students who submit feedback, 
teachers who receive feedback and analytics, and administrators who manage the 
entire ecosystem. The system handles the complete data hierarchy of a 
university: departments contain semesters, semesters contain sections, sections 
contain subjects, and subjects are taught by teachers who are assigned to 
specific sections.

The feedback mechanism allows students to rate teachers on a scale of 1 to 10 
and provide written comments. Students can subsequently edit their feedback, 
with version tracking maintaining an audit trail. Teachers can view aggregated 
ratings broken down by subject, view AI-generated summaries of their 
performance, and engage in AI-powered chat conversations for coaching and 
insights.

Administrators can view teacher rankings, track student feedback completion 
status, monitor pending students who have not completed all their feedback, 
manage user accounts (create, update, delete, reset passwords), configure AI 
settings including API keys and model selection, and chat with AI about 
individual teacher performance.

The system includes a multi-step setup wizard for initial system configuration 
covering departments, semesters, sections, subjects, teachers, assignments, 
students, and CSV bulk upload.

However, certain limitations exist. The system relies on Google Gemini API for 
AI functionality, which requires internet connectivity and incurs usage costs 
beyond free tier limits. The system does not include support for anonymous 
feedback at the database level as student identities are tracked for submission 
verification, though teacher-facing views aggregate data. The system is designed 
for university-scale deployment and may require optimization for extremely large 
institutions with hundreds of thousands of students. Mobile applications are 
not provided, though the responsive web design ensures usability on mobile 
browsers.

2.1 Evolution of Student Feedback Systems

The practice of collecting student feedback on teaching performance dates back 
several decades and has evolved significantly with technological advancements. 
Early systems relied on paper-based questionnaires that were distributed in 
classrooms, collected manually, and analyzed through labor-intensive tabulation 
processes. These traditional methods, while straightforward, suffered from 
numerous limitations including low response rates due to student apathy and 
distribution inefficiencies, manual data entry errors introduced during the 
transcription process, significantly delayed results that could take weeks or 
months to compile and disseminate, and the substantial environmental impact of 
paper consumption across thousands of forms per academic term. Additionally, 
paper-based systems offered limited anonymity, which often discouraged honest 
and critical feedback from students who feared identification through 
handwriting recognition or other means.

The advent of web technologies in the 1990s and early 2000s brought about 
online survey platforms that digitized the feedback collection process. 
Institutions began adopting tools like SurveyMonkey, Google Forms, and 
custom-built web applications to collect student feedback electronically. These 
digital solutions improved efficiency in data collection by eliminating physical 
distribution and manual data entry, reduced turnaround times for result 
compilation, and provided better anonymity through digital submission channels. 
However, these early digital solutions often remained disconnected from 
institutional systems, requiring separate login credentials, manual data export 
processes, and integration work to connect feedback data with existing student 
information systems and learning management platforms.

Modern feedback systems have evolved into comprehensive educational management 
platforms that integrate feedback collection with other institutional functions. 
Learning Management Systems like Canvas, Blackboard, and Moodle have 
incorporated feedback modules, enabling seamless integration with course 
management, grade books, and student enrollment data. These integrated solutions 
provide the advantage of contextualized feedback that can be correlated with 
academic performance metrics and course completion rates. However, these 
integrated solutions often provide limited advanced analytics capabilities, 
typically offering only basic statistical measures such as mean, median, and 
standard deviation calculations. They lack artificial intelligence features for 
qualitative data analysis, sentiment detection from written comments, and 
automated insight generation that can transform raw feedback into actionable 
teaching improvement recommendations.

The University Teachers Feedback System represents the next generation of 
feedback platforms by combining modern web technologies with artificial 
intelligence to deliver insights that go beyond simple statistical aggregation. 
The system addresses the limitations of both traditional and current digital 
solutions by providing a unified platform that incorporates AI-powered analysis 
of qualitative feedback, intelligent caching for performance optimization, 
comprehensive role-based interfaces tailored to each stakeholder group, and 
streamlined data management through bulk import capabilities.

2.2 AI in Educational Technology

Artificial intelligence has emerged as a transformative force in educational 
technology, with applications ranging from personalized learning paths to 
automated grading systems. Natural language processing, in particular, has 
enabled machines to understand, interpret, and generate human language in ways 
that were previously impossible.

Google Gemini, the AI model powering this system, represents the state of the 
art in large language models. Gemini can understand complex instructions, 
analyze large volumes of text, generate coherent summaries, and engage in 
natural conversation. When applied to student feedback analysis, these 
capabilities enable the automatic generation of teacher performance summaries 
that synthesize dozens or hundreds of individual comments into concise, 
actionable insights.

The use of AI in feedback analysis offers several advantages over traditional 
manual methods. First, AI analysis is consistent and reproducible, applying the 
same analytical standards across all feedback data. Second, AI can process large 
volumes of data in seconds, a task that would take human analysts hours or days. 
Third, AI can identify patterns and themes that might be missed by human 
analysts, particularly when dealing with large datasets. Fourth, AI-generated 
summaries are objective and unbiased, avoiding the subjective interpretations 
that can influence human analysis.

The system employs Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash and Flash Lite models, which offer 
an optimal balance of performance, speed, and cost for the specific task of 
feedback analysis. The Lite variant is particularly well-suited for high-volume, 
lower-complexity tasks, while the full Flash model can be selected for more 
nuanced analysis when needed.

2.3 Existing Solutions and Their Limitations

Several commercial and open-source solutions exist for collecting and analyzing 
student feedback. Learning Management Systems like Canvas and Blackboard offer 
built-in survey and evaluation tools that allow instructors to create and 
distribute feedback forms. However, these tools are typically designed for 
course-level evaluation rather than institution-wide teacher assessment, and 
they lack advanced analytics capabilities.

Dedicated student evaluation platforms like EvaluationKit, Campus Labs, and 
Blue by Explorance offer more sophisticated features specifically designed for 
student feedback collection. These platforms provide comprehensive survey 
creation tools, automated distribution, and detailed reporting. However, they 
are commercial products with significant licensing costs, and they may not offer 
the level of customization required by specific institutions.

Open-source solutions like OMBEA and LimeSurvey provide alternatives for 
institutions with limited budgets, but they require significant technical 
expertise for deployment and customization, and they typically lack integrated 
AI capabilities.

The common limitations of existing solutions include: absence of AI-powered 
analysis for qualitative comments; limited role-based interfaces tailored to 
students, teachers, and administrators; lack of integrated progress tracking 
for feedback completion; inadequate support for bulk data import; and no 
provision for AI-driven teacher coaching through chat interfaces. The 
University Teachers Feedback System addresses all these limitations through 
its comprehensive design.

2.4 Modern Web Technologies for Education

The selection of appropriate technologies is crucial for the success of any 
educational software system. The University Teachers Feedback System employs a 
modern technology stack chosen for its performance, developer experience, and 
ecosystem support.

React, the frontend library developed by Facebook, has become the dominant 
choice for building user interfaces due to its component-based architecture, 
efficient virtual DOM rendering, and extensive ecosystem. Combined with Vite, a 
next-generation build tool that offers instant server start and fast hot module 
replacement, the frontend delivers a responsive user experience with rapid 
development iteration.

Node.js provides the backend runtime, offering excellent performance for 
I/O-intensive applications typical of web APIs. Express, the most popular 
Node.js web framework, provides a minimalist yet powerful foundation for 
building RESTful APIs with middleware support and routing capabilities.

MongoDB, a NoSQL document database, was selected for its flexible schema design 
that accommodates the hierarchical data structures common in educational 
institutions. Mongoose provides an elegant object modeling interface for MongoDB 
with schema validation, middleware, and query building capabilities.

TailwindCSS offers a utility-first CSS framework that enables rapid interface 
development with consistent design patterns. Combined with React, it enables 
the creation of responsive, accessible, and visually appealing user interfaces.

Google Gemini AI provides the artificial intelligence backbone, offering 
state-of-the-art natural language processing capabilities through a simple API 
interface. The integration of AI directly into the feedback analysis pipeline 
represents a significant advancement over traditional feedback systems.

FR5: The system shall protect all API endpoints based on user roles.

FR7: The system shall show feedback submission status for each teacher

     feedback.

FR11: The system shall display a progress bar showing overall feedback 
      completion percentage.

      the teacher.

FR14: The system shall generate a rating distribution (1-10) for each subject.
FR15: The system shall display overall average rating across all subjects.
FR16: The system shall generate AI-powered summaries of student feedback.
FR17: The system shall cache AI summaries and regenerate only when new feedback

FR29: The system shall allow management of multiple Gemini API keys with

FR31: The system shall allow administrators to chat with AI about individual

Setup Wizard:

FR37: The system shall allow registration of teacher accounts.
FR38: The system shall allow assignment of teachers to subjects within sections.
FR39: The system shall allow single student registration with all details.
FR40: The system shall support bulk student import via CSV file upload.

FR42: The system shall automatically resolve username and CNIC conflicts during

NFR8: API keys stored in the database must be protected.

Scalability:
NFR10: The system architecture should support horizontal scaling.
NFR11: The database connection should be cached for serverless environments.

Reliability:
NFR13: The system should handle API failures gracefully with appropriate error

NFR14: AI rate limiting should be managed with retry-after mechanisms.
NFR15: The system should validate all input data before processing.

Usability:
NFR16: The interface should be responsive and work on mobile devices.
NFR17: The system should support dark mode and light mode themes.
NFR18: The interface should provide clear feedback for all user actions.
NFR19: Navigation should be intuitive with clear role-based menus.

Maintainability:
NFR20: Code should be modular with clear separation of concerns.

The system implements three distinct user roles, each with specific permissions:

Student Role:
- Can view own profile and assigned teachers
- Can submit feedback on assigned teachers
- Can edit own feedback
- Can view own feedback history
- Cannot view other students' data
- Cannot view teacher analytics
- Cannot access admin functions

Teacher Role:
- Can view own ratings and analytics
- Can view AI-generated summaries of own performance
- Can chat with AI about own feedback
- Cannot view other teachers' data
- Cannot access student feedback in identifiable form
- Cannot access admin functions

Administrator Role:
- Can view all system data including teacher rankings

- Can access setup wizard for system configuration
- Can configure AI settings

- Can chat with AI about any teacher's performance
- Cannot submit feedback as a student
- Cannot view data as a teacher

4.1 Overall System Architecture

The University Teachers Feedback System follows a modern client-server 
architecture with clear separation between the frontend presentation layer and 
the backend API layer. The architecture is designed for deployment on Vercel's 
serverless platform, which influences several design decisions.

The system architecture consists of three main layers:

1. Frontend Layer: A React single-page application (SPA) built with Vite that 
   runs in the user's browser. The frontend handles all user interface rendering, 
   client-side routing, authentication state management, and API communication 
   through Axios HTTP client with JWT interceptor.

2. Backend API Layer: A Node.js Express application that handles all business 
   logic, data validation, authentication, authorization, and AI integration. 
   The API exposes RESTful endpoints organized by functional domain (auth, 
   setup, student, feedback, teacher, admin).

3. Database Layer: MongoDB Atlas provides the data persistence layer. Mongoose 
   ODM is used for schema definition, validation, query building, and data 
   modeling. The database is organized into 11 collections that model the 
   complete educational domain.

The architecture also integrates with external services:
- Google Gemini AI API for natural language processing
- MongoDB Atlas for database hosting
- Vercel for hosting both frontend and backend

4.2 Frontend Architecture

The frontend follows React's component-based architecture with a focus on 
modularity, reusability, and separation of concerns. The application structure 
is organized as follows:

Root Level:
- App.tsx: Main application component that sets up routing, theme, and 
  authentication providers
- main.tsx: Application entry point that renders the App component
- index.css: Global styles and TailwindCSS directives
- App.css: Application-level styles

Context Layer:
- AuthContext: Manages authentication state including user info, tokens, login, 
  and logout functions. Hydrates state from localStorage on initial load and 
  provides the authentication context to all child components.
- ThemeContext: Manages theme state (light/dark mode) with persistence to 
  localStorage.

Component Layer:
- auth/: Authentication components including LoginForm and ProtectedRoute
- student/: Student dashboard components including Dashboard, TeacherCard, 
  FeedbackForm, and FeedbackBadge
- teacher/: Teacher dashboard components including Dashboard, RatingChart, 
  SummaryPanel, and AIChat
- admin/: Admin dashboard components including Dashboard, TeacherRankingTable, 
  RankChart, StudentTracking, PendingStudents, Management, and AISettings
- setup/: Setup wizard component
- common/: Shared components including ThemeToggle and FormattedMessage

API Layer:
- api/client.ts: Axios instance with base URL configuration, JWT interceptor

4.5 AI Integration Architecture

The AI integration is designed with reliability, cost optimization, and 
scalability in mind. The architecture follows these principles:

Multi-Key Rotation: The system supports configuration of multiple Gemini API 
keys. When a request is made, the system iterates through active keys until one 
succeeds. Failed keys are automatically deactivated if the error indicates an 
invalid key, and success/failure is tracked per key for monitoring.

Caching Strategy: AI summaries are cached in the AISummaries collection. When a 
teacher requests their summary, the system checks the cached version against the 
current feedback count. If the count matches, the cached summary is returned 
immediately. If new feedback exists, a stale indicator is shown and the teacher 
can request regeneration. This approach reduces API costs and improves response 
times.

Rate Limiting: The system implements rate limiting with cooldown periods. When 
a 429 (rate limit) error is received from Gemini, the system calculates the 
retry delay from the error response and prevents further summary generation 
requests for that teacher during the cooldown period.

Context Handling: For chat functionality, the system manages conversation 
history with intelligent context management. When the history exceeds a length 
threshold, the system uses Gemini itself to summarize the previous conversation, 
compacting it into a dense representation. This prevents token limit issues 
while preserving conversation continuity.

Model Selection: Administrators can choose between two Gemini models: 
gemini-2.5-flash for higher quality analysis and gemini-2.5-flash-lite for 
faster, more cost-effective processing. The Lite model is the default for its 
optimal balance of performance and cost.

5.3.2 Authentication and Authorization

Authentication is implemented using JWT (JSON Web Tokens) with the following 
flow:

1. User submits credentials (username and password) to /api/auth/login.
2. Server validates credentials against the database using bcrypt comparison.
3. On success, server generates:
   - Access token: Short-lived JWT (15 minutes) containing user ID and role
   - Refresh token: Longer-lived JWT (7 days) containing user ID
4. Server returns both tokens along with user profile information.
5. Client stores tokens in localStorage and attaches the access token to 
   subsequent API requests via Axios interceptor.
6. When the access token expires, the Axios response interceptor automatically 
   attempts to refresh it using the refresh token.
7. If refresh succeeds, the new access token is stored and the original request 
   is retried.
8. If refresh fails, the client is redirected to the login page.

Authorization is implemented through two middleware layers:

1. Authentication Middleware: Verifies the JWT access token from the 
   Authorization header. On success, decodes the token and attaches user 
   information (id, role) to the request object. On failure, returns 401 
   Unauthorized.

2. Role-Based Access Control Middleware: Checks the user's role (from the 
   authenticated request) against a list of allowed roles for the route. On 
   failure, returns 403 Forbidden.

5.3.3 Middleware Layer

The middleware layer consists of three custom middlewares:

Auth Middleware (middleware/auth.ts):
- Extracts the JWT from the Authorization header (Bearer token format)
- Verifies the token using the JWT secret
- If valid, attaches decoded payload (id, role, iat, exp) to req.user
- If invalid or expired, returns 401 Unauthorized response
- Used on all protected routes

RBAC Middleware (middleware/rbac.ts):
- Factory function that takes allowedRoles array
- Returns middleware that checks req.user.role against allowedRoles
- If role matches, calls next()
- If role doesn't match, returns 403 Forbidden response
- Used on role-specific routes

Upload Middleware (middleware/upload.ts):
- Configures Multer for file upload handling
- Uses memory storage to store uploaded file in buffer
- Accepts single file uploads with field name 'file'
- Filters for CSV file type
- Used on the bulk CSV upload endpoint

5.3.4 Route Handlers

Each route handler follows a consistent pattern:

1. Input validation: Check required fields from request body, query parameters, 
   or URL parameters. Return 400 Bad Request if required fields are missing.

2. Business logic: Execute the core operation including database queries, 
   service calls, and data transformations.

3. Error handling: Wrap operations in try-catch blocks. Handle specific error 
   types:
   - 11000 (MongoDB duplicate key error): Return 409 Conflict with descriptive 
     message
   - Validation errors: Return 400 Bad Request
   - Not found errors: Return 404 Not Found
   - Authorization errors: Return 403 Forbidden
   - Generic errors: Log the error and return 500 Internal Server Error

4. Response formatting: Return consistent JSON responses with appropriate HTTP 
   status codes (200 for success, 201 for creation, etc.)

The admin route handler is the most complex, implementing endpoints for:
- Teacher rankings with aggregation
- Student tracking with pagination
- Pending students identification
- AI settings management (CRUD for API keys)
- User management (CRUD, password reset, deletion with cascading data cleanup)
- AI chat for teacher performance analysis

This collection caches AI-generated summaries per teacher. The feedbackCount

Schema fields: teacherId (ref: User, required, unique), messages (array of

5.5 AI Service Implementation

5.5.1 Gemini Integration

The Gemini integration is implemented through a custom service layer that 
provides abstraction over the Google Generative AI SDK. The key design 
decisions include:

Dynamic Key Selection: The service fetches active API keys from the AISettings 
collection at runtime. This allows administrators to add, remove, or deactivate 
keys without restarting the server. Keys are stored in the database with labels 
for identification, masked in responses for security.

Key Rotation and Failover: When making an API call, the service iterates 
through all active keys. If a key fails (due to rate limiting, quota exhaustion, 
or invalid key), the service automatically tries the next key. Failed keys are 
marked with the error details and timestamp. Keys that fail with "API key not 
valid" errors are automatically deactivated.

Success Tracking: Successful API calls update the key's lastUsedAt timestamp 
and clear any previous error state. This provides administrators with visibility 
into which keys are functioning correctly.

Error Typing: The service categorizes errors for appropriate handling:
- Invalid API key errors automatically deactivate the key
- Rate limit (429) errors trigger cooldown mechanisms
- Network and transient errors are logged and cause key rotation

5.5.2 Summary Generation

The teacher summary generation process follows these steps:

1. The teacher requests their summary via GET /api/teacher/summary
2. The system fetches all teacher assignments for the authenticated teacher
3. The system queries the Feedback collection for all feedback linked to those 
   assignments
4. Comments and ratings are extracted from the feedback documents
5. The collected data is sent to Gemini with a carefully crafted prompt that 
   instructs the AI to:
   - Analyze student sentiment
   - Identify key strengths
   - Highlight areas for improvement
   - Synthesize into 3-4 concise sentences
   - Not invent details not present in the comments
6. The generated summary is cached in the AISummaries collection with the 
   current feedback count
7. On subsequent requests, if the feedback count hasn't changed, the cached 
   summary is returned immediately

The prompt engineering for summary generation is critical for quality results:
- Average rating and total response count provide numerical context
- Individual student comments are listed with quotes for reference
- The AI is instructed to be specific and grounded in the provided data
- A length constraint (3-4 sentences) ensures concise summaries

5.5.4 Key Management and Rotation

The key management system provides administrators with full control over AI 
configuration:

Adding Keys: Administrators can add new API keys with descriptive labels. Keys 
are stored securely in the database.

Viewing Keys: Keys are always masked in API responses, showing only the first 
4 and last 4 characters for identification. This prevents accidental exposure 
of full keys.

Updating Keys: Administrators can update key labels, replace keys with new 
values, and toggle key active status.

Deleting Keys: Keys can be permanently removed from the system.

Model Selection: Administrators can switch between Gemini 2.5 Flash and Flash 
Lite models. The selection applies to all AI operations including summary 
generation and chat.

Key Monitoring: Each key tracks its last used timestamp, last failure timestamp, 
and last error message. This information helps administrators identify issues 
with specific keys.

Key Rotation: At runtime, the AI service iterates through all active keys. This 
rotation provides:
- Load balancing across multiple API keys
- Automatic failover when a key fails
- Graceful degradation when some keys are exhausted
- Centralized management without code changes

6.5 Security Features

6.5.1 JWT Authentication

The system implements JSON Web Token authentication with the following design:

Access Tokens:
- Short-lived (15 minutes default)
- Contain user ID and role in the payload
- Signed with a strong secret key
- Sent with every API request in the Authorization header
- Validated on every protected endpoint

Refresh Tokens:
- Longer-lived (7 days default)
- Contain user ID in the payload
- Signed with a separate refresh secret key
- Used only for obtaining new access tokens
- Stored in localStorage for client-side persistence

Token Refresh Flow:
1. Axios interceptor detects 401 response
2. Interceptor sends refresh token to /api/auth/refresh-token
3. Server validates refresh token and verifies user still exists
4. Server returns new access token
5. Interceptor retries the original request with new token
6. If refresh fails, user is logged out and redirected to login

6.5.2 Role-Based Access Control

Access control is implemented at multiple levels:

Frontend Level:
- ProtectedRoute component wraps role-specific routes
- Routes are only accessible if user role matches allowed roles
- Unauthorized access redirects to /unauthorized page

Backend Level:
- Authentication middleware validates JWT on every request
- RBAC middleware checks user role against required roles
- Each route handler verifies data ownership (e.g., student can only access 
  own feedback)
- Server responses include 401 for unauthenticated and 403 for unauthorized 
  requests

6.5.3 Password Hashing

Passwords are secured using bcrypt:

Hashing Algorithm:
- Uses bcryptjs library implementation
- Salts are automatically generated and incorporated into the hash
- Configurable cost factor for performance/security balance

Storage:
- Only hashed passwords are stored in the database
- Original passwords are never persisted
- Password comparison uses bcrypt's built-in comparison function

6.5.4 Token Refresh Mechanism

The token refresh mechanism is designed for security and reliability:

Client-side Implementation:
- Axios response interceptor catches 401 errors
- Uses a retry flag (_retry) to prevent infinite loops
- Sends refresh token to server in a POST request
- Updates access token in localStorage and retries original request
- On failure, clears all stored data and redirects to login

Server-side Implementation:
- Validates refresh token signature and expiration
- Verifies the user still exists in the database
- Generates a new access token with current user role
- Returns the new token to the client

7.1 Entity Relationship Overview

The database design follows a hierarchical structure that mirrors the 
organizational structure of a typical university:

Department (1) -> Semester (N) -> Section (N) -> Subject (N)
                                                      |
                                                      v
User(T) TeacherAssignment(N) -> Subject(1)
  ^         |
  |         v
  +--- Feedback(N) -> Student(1) -> User(S)
                                   |
                                   +-> Section(1)
                                   +-> Semester(1)

Key relationships:
- Each department has multiple semesters
- Each semester has multiple sections
- Each section has multiple subjects
- Teachers are users with role 'teacher'
- Teacher assignments connect teachers to subjects in specific sections/
  semesters
- Students are users with role 'student', pinned to a section and semester
- Feedback connects students to teacher assignments with ratings and comments
- AI summaries cache per teacher
- Chat sessions store per-teacher conversation history

7.3 Indexes and Performance Optimization

The database indexes are designed to support the most common query patterns:

Authentication Queries:
- User lookup by username during login: { username: 1 } unique index

Student Assignment Queries:
- Finding assignments for a student's section and semester: 
  { sectionId: 1, semesterId: 1 } compound index
- Finding a student's profile by userId: { userId: 1 } index
- Finding a student by section: { sectionId: 1, semesterId: 1 } compound index

Feedback Queries:
- Checking if feedback exists for a student-assignment pair: 
  { studentId: 1, assignmentId: 1 } compound unique index
- Finding all feedback for an assignment: { assignmentId: 1 } index
- Finding all feedback by a student: { studentId: 1 } index

Teacher Queries:
- Finding all assignments for a teacher: { teacherId: 1 } index
- Finding AI summary for a teacher: { teacherId: 1 } unique index
- Finding chat session for a teacher: { teacherId: 1 } unique index
- Finding teachers by role: no special index needed since role distribution is 
  small

Summary Caching:
- Efficient upsert by teacherId: { teacherId: 1 } unique index

The sparse index on students.cnic allows multiple null values while maintaining 
uniqueness for provided CNIC values, accommodating students who may not have a 
CNIC.

               9. TESTING AND QUALITY ASSURANCE
================================================================================

9.1 Testing Strategies

The University Teachers Feedback System employs a multi-layered approach to 
quality assurance:

Input Validation: All API endpoints validate input data before processing. 
Required fields are checked and missing fields result in clear error messages 
with 400 status codes. Data types and formats are validated where applicable.

Error Handling: Comprehensive try-catch blocks wrap all request handlers. 
Specific error types are caught and handled appropriately:
- MongoDB duplicate key errors (code 11000) return 409 Conflict
- Validation errors return 400 Bad Request
- Not found conditions return 404 Not Found
- Authorization failures return 403 Forbidden
- Generic errors are logged and return 500 Internal Server Error

Database Seed Scripts: A seed.ts script populates the database with test data 
including departments, semesters, sections, subjects, teachers, students, and 
assignments. This enables consistent testing across development environments.

Data Verification Scripts: Utility scripts (check-passwords.ts, list-users.ts, 
reset-all-passwords.ts, generate-csv.ts) provide tools for verifying database 
state and troubleshooting issues.

9.2 Error Handling

The system implements comprehensive error handling at multiple levels:

API Level: Each route handler wraps its logic in try-catch blocks. Errors are 
logged to the console with context (endpoint name, error message, stack trace) 
for debugging. Appropriate HTTP status codes are returned to the client.

AI Service Level: The Gemini service implements sophisticated error handling:
- Invalid API key detection with automatic key deactivation
- Rate limit (429) detection with cooldown period management
- Key rotation on failure
- Clear error messages for administrators about key configuration issues
- Fallback responses when AI generation fails

Client Level: The frontend handles errors at multiple levels:
- Axios interceptor for automatic token refresh on 401
- Component-level error states with user-friendly messages
- Loading states that prevent double submissions
- Network error handling for connectivity issues

Database Level: Mongoose provides schema validation that catches data integrity 
issues before they reach the database. Unique indexes prevent duplicate records.

9.3 Performance Considerations

The system incorporates several performance optimizations:

Database Indexing: Strategic indexes on frequently queried fields and compound 
indexes on common query patterns ensure efficient query execution. Unique 
indexes provide data integrity while improving lookup performance.

Connection Caching: For serverless deployment, the MongoDB connection is cached 
across function invocations, avoiding the overhead of establishing a new 
connection for each request.

AI Summary Caching: The most expensive operation (AI summary generation) is 
cached with feedback count tracking. This reduces Gemini API calls by 
approximately 90% for returning teachers, as most summary requests are served 
from cache.

Batch Processing: The admin endpoints that process multiple students or teachers 
use batch operations and avoid N+1 query patterns. For example, the pending 
students endpoint fetches all feedback records once and builds a lookup map 
rather than querying per student.

Optimized Query Patterns: Mongoose queries use .select() to fetch only 
necessary fields and .lean() for read-only operations to reduce memory overhead. 
Population is used judiciously to avoid over-fetching related data.

Responsive Frontend: The frontend uses React's efficient rendering with proper 
key props, conditional rendering for loading states, and optimized re-renders 
through state management patterns.

10.1 Deployment Architecture

The system is designed for deployment on Vercel's serverless platform:

Frontend Deployment:
- Built as a static site using Vite's build process
- Deployed to Vercel's global CDN
- Environment variables configured in Vercel dashboard
- Automatic HTTPS and domain management

Backend Deployment:
- Deployed as serverless functions on Vercel
- Each API route becomes a serverless function
- Connection pooling managed through connection caching
- Automatic scaling based on traffic

Database:
- MongoDB Atlas cluster for managed database hosting
- Automatic backups and monitoring
- Replica sets for high availability
- IP whitelisting for security

The deployment architecture provides:
- Global availability through CDN distribution
- Automatic scaling from zero to production traffic
- Zero-downtime deployments through Vercel's preview deployments
- Cost efficiency through pay-per-execution serverless pricing

10.2 Environment Configuration

The system uses environment variables for configuration:

Backend (.env):
- PORT: Server port (default: 5000)
- MONGO_URI: MongoDB connection string
- JWT_SECRET: Secret key for access token signing
- JWT_REFRESH_SECRET: Secret key for refresh token signing
- GEMINI_API_KEY: Default Gemini API key (fallback when no keys configured in 
  database)
- VERCEL: Set to '1' when deployed on Vercel (disables app.listen)

Frontend (.env):
- VITE_API_URL: Backend API URL for production deployment

10.3 Vercel Deployment

The system is configured for Vercel deployment:

Backend vercel.json:
{
  "version": 2,
  "builds": [{ "src": "api/index.ts", "use": "@vercel/node" }],
  "routes": [{ "src": "/api/(.*)", "dest": "api/index.ts" }]
}

The api/index.ts file exports the Express app as a serverless function handler.
Vercel automatically manages the serverless function lifecycle, including cold 
starts and scaling.

Frontend vercel.json:
{
  "rewrites": [{ "source": "/(.*)", "destination": "/index.html" }]
}
This configuration ensures that client-side routing works correctly on Vercel 
by redirecting all routes to the index.html entry point.

10.4 MongoDB Atlas Integration

MongoDB Atlas provides the production database infrastructure:

Connection: The system connects to MongoDB Atlas using a connection string that 
includes:
- Username and password for authentication
- Shard addresses for replica set discovery
- SSL/TLS enabled for encrypted communication
- Write concern configured for data durability
- Retry writes enabled for fault tolerance

Performance Configuration:
- Server selection timeout: 5000ms (fail fast on cold starts)
- Socket timeout: 45000ms (accommodate long-running queries)
- Connection pool: Managed by Mongoose with size appropriate for serverless

                         12. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

The University Teachers Feedback System represents a significant advancement in 
educational technology by combining modern web application architecture with 
artificial intelligence to transform the student feedback process. The system 
successfully addresses the limitations of traditional feedback mechanisms 
through a comprehensive, integrated platform that serves the needs of students, 
teachers, and administrators.

The system's architecture, built on React, Node.js, Express, and MongoDB, 
provides a robust, scalable, and maintainable foundation. The integration of 
Google Gemini AI enables automatic generation of teacher performance summaries, 
intelligent chatbot coaching, and advanced analytics that would be impractical 
with manual analysis methods. The multi-key rotation system ensures reliable AI 
service availability while the caching mechanism optimizes API usage and reduces 
costs.

The role-based access control implementation ensures that each user type has 
appropriate views and capabilities, maintaining data security and privacy. 
Students benefit from an intuitive interface for submitting and editing feedback 
with real-time progress tracking. Teachers gain access to comprehensive rating 
analytics, AI-generated performance insights, and an interactive coaching 
chatbot. Administrators have complete visibility into the system through 
overview statistics, teacher rankings, student tracking, and user management 
tools.

The comprehensive setup wizard simplifies initial system configuration, and the 
CSV bulk import capability enables rapid population of the database with 
student records. The caching strategy for AI summaries significantly reduces 
response times and API costs. The responsive design ensures the system is 
accessible on mobile devices, while the dark mode theme provides a comfortable 
user experience in various lighting conditions.

The system's deployment on Vercel's serverless platform provides global 
availability, automatic scaling, and cost-effective operations. MongoDB Atlas 
ensures reliable, managed database hosting with built-in backup and monitoring 
capabilities.

In conclusion, the University Teachers Feedback System provides a complete, 
production-ready solution for educational institutions seeking to modernize 
their feedback collection and analysis processes. The combination of intuitive 
user interfaces, comprehensive analytics, and AI-powered insights creates a 
platform that not only streamlines administrative processes but also provides 
genuine value to teachers through actionable performance insights and to 
students through an engaging feedback experience.
